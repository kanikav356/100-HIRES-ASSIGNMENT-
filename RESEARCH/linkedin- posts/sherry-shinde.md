I CRACKED the LinkedIn CODE.

Want more people clicking "See More"?

Stop obsessing over the perfect words.
Start obsessing over the 𝘳𝘪𝘨𝘩𝘵 reaction.

Every high-performing hook triggers one of six forces:

1. 𝗖𝘂𝗿𝗶𝗼𝘀𝗶𝘁𝘆 — creates a gap between what they know and what they want to know

2. 𝗦𝘂𝗿𝗽𝗿𝗶𝘀𝗲 — violates an expectation their brain was already predicting

3. 𝗜𝗱𝗲𝗻𝘁𝗶𝘁𝘆 — makes them see themselves inside your words

4. 𝗘𝗺𝗼𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 — signals to the brain that this information matters

5. 𝗗𝗲𝘀𝗶𝗿𝗲 — points toward something they already deeply want

6. 𝗧𝗲𝗻𝘀𝗶𝗼𝗻 — introduces instability the brain desperately needs to resolve
 
Most weak hooks activate zero of these.

They look like this:

❌ "3 lessons I learned building my startup"

❌ "Here's what happened to me last week"

❌ "I've been thinking about something lately"

These are written from the creator's perspective.

Strong hooks are written from the audience's psychology.

Compare:

❌ "Three lessons about personal branding"

✅ "I asked 7 founders who scaled to $1M without a personal brand the same question. They all gave the same answer."

Same topic.

But the second creates tension, curiosity, and a question the reader physically cannot answer without clicking "𝘮𝘰𝘳𝘦."

Before you hit publish, run every hook through this filter...

I call it CPR:

𝗖 — 𝗖𝘂𝗿𝗶𝗼𝘀𝗶𝘁𝘆

Is there a missing piece they need to find? If the reader already has the answer after reading your hook, there's no reason to continue.

𝗣 — 𝗣𝗿𝗲𝗰𝗶𝘀𝗶𝗼𝗻

Is it specific or vague? "I lost $20,000 on a bad hire" lands infinitely harder than "I made a costly mistake." Specificity creates credibility.

𝗥 — 𝗥𝗲𝗹𝗲𝘃𝗮𝗻𝗰𝗲

Does your audience genuinely care?

A hook can be curious and specific but if it doesn't connect to a real frustration or desire your reader already carries, it still won't work.

Miss even one of these and the hook dies before the content gets a chance.

Instead of: "What do I want to say?"

Ask: "What conversation is already happening in my reader's mind and how do I enter it?"

Because great hooks don't start where the story starts.

They start where interest starts.

And the hook was never the beginning of your content.

It's the beginning of attention.

** Annotation: Sherry introduces a practical framework for crafting effective LinkedIn hooks by focusing on audience psychology instead of clever wording. She identifies six emotional triggers—curiosity, surprise, identity, emotion, desire, and tension—and presents the CPR framework (Curiosity, Precision, Relevance) as a checklist for evaluating every opening line.

** Original Post Content: 
🚨 The Algorithm has CHANGED. 🚨

LinkedIn's algorithm has one core job:

Predict what will keep someone on the platform longer.

So it doesn't ask "Is this good content?"

It asks: "Does this person spark conversations that bring people back?"

That's a completely different question.

Here's what that means in practice:

A post that gets 200 likes and zero comments?

The algorithm treats it like a billboard.

Seen. Forgotten. Filed. ❌

A post that gets 40 likes and 60 comments?

The algorithm treats it like a campfire.

People keep coming back to see what happened next.

The metric you're not tracking is return engagement.

People who comment, leave, then come back to reply again.

That's the signal that tells LinkedIn:

"This person creates gravity."

And gravity is what compounds.

So stop asking "How do I write better posts?"

Start asking: "How do I write posts that people feel compelled to respond to twice?"

The answer is almost always the same:

Take a clear stance. Invite disagreement.
Then actually show up in the comments.

This is not a content strategy.
This is a PRESENCE STRATEGY.

And presence is what converts.

** Annotation: LinkedIn's algorithm prioritizes meaningful discussions and repeat engagement over simple reactions. Sherry argues that posts should encourage conversation through clear opinions, thoughtful prompts, and active participation in the comments. The concept of a "presence strategy" highlights that engagement continues after publishing and contributes to long-term visibility.

If you want people to actually stop scrolling on your posts…

Stop giving them the answer right away.

Give them the itch.

Okay, wait, look at this:

“𝘐 𝘭𝘰𝘴𝘵 50𝘒 𝘪𝘯 2023 𝘣𝘺 𝘵𝘳𝘶𝘴𝘵𝘪𝘯𝘨 𝘵𝘩𝘦 𝘸𝘳𝘰𝘯𝘨 𝘢𝘥𝘷𝘪𝘤𝘦
𝘛𝘩𝘢𝘵 𝘮𝘪𝘴𝘵𝘢𝘬𝘦 𝘮𝘢𝘥𝘦 𝘮𝘦 𝘴𝘦𝘷𝘦𝘯 𝘧𝘪𝘨𝘶𝘳𝘦𝘴 𝘵𝘩𝘦 𝘯𝘦𝘹𝘵 𝘺𝘦𝘢𝘳.”

You see, the opening “I lost 50K” immediately grabs attention.

The word “Loss” is emotionally charged and humans are wired to notice pain, risk, and failure

The audience reacts instantly:

“𝗪𝗮𝗶𝘁, 𝟱𝟬𝗞? 𝗧𝗵𝗮𝘁’𝘀 𝗵𝘂𝗴𝗲! 𝗪𝗵𝗮𝘁 𝗵𝗮𝗽𝗽𝗲𝗻𝗲𝗱?”

“That mistake made me seven figures the next year”

This line teases a transformation, but doesn’t reveal the how.

Now the brain wants closure:

“𝗪𝗮𝗶𝘁. 𝗛𝗼𝘄 𝗱𝗶𝗱 𝗮 𝗺𝗮𝘀𝘀𝗶𝘃𝗲 𝗹𝗼𝘀𝘀 𝘁𝘂𝗿𝗻 𝗶𝗻𝘁𝗼 𝗺𝗮𝘀𝘀𝗶𝘃𝗲 𝗴𝗮𝗶𝗻?”

This creates a mental loop that the reader is compelled to resolve.

This is called a curiosity gap.

Leave just enough mystery so your audience leans in, thinks, and clicks ‘see more.'

** Annotation: By introducing a compelling problem and delaying the resolution, creators can trigger curiosity and improve engagement.
