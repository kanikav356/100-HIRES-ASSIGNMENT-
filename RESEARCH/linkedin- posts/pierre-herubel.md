** Original Post Content: 
The Demand Gen playbook relies on a simple equation:

(If the equation is unbalanced, the model fails)

You need to balance demand creation and demand capture.

Otherwise, you'll fall into one of those 2 scenarios:

Scenario 1: Too much demand creation and not enough demand capture investment

The business invests a lot in demand generation activities (podcasts, socials, reports...). But the demand capture system is not robust enough (CRM is not segmented, pages are not optimized...) so there are revenue leaks. 

Scenario 2: Not enough demand creation and too much demand capture investment

The business creates a robust demand capture system (everything is perfect). But they don't generate enough demand because there are not enough activities (content, awareness ads, partnerships...)

I've seen both scenarios in my career.

- Businesses with a strong sales culture often fall into scenario 2.
- Whereas those with a strong marketing culture fall into scenario 1. 

But the good news is that:

Both scenarios are fixable with the right playbook.

Here are the 2 steps you need to install:

Step 1 - Create 2 types of content for passive buyers

~95% of your target market is not in "buying mode". But most of them are always in "learning mode": they want to keep up to date with their industry and job requirements.

- Type 1: Zero-click content makes your brand discoverable 
- Type 2: Gated content delivers deeper expertise

Step 2 - Build a dynamic demand capture system

~5% are in "research or buying mode" but it doesn't necessarily mean they'll become inbound leads. They might prefer competitors or forget to contact you.

- Tactic 1: Conversion content to turn audience into leads
- Tactic 2: Warm Outbound to start conversations

I'm adding more relevant resources in the comments to help you do this.

** Annotation: LinkedIn organic content should not exist in isolation but as part of a complete demand generation system. Pierre explains that awareness-building content (zero-click posts and educational resources) must be complemented by demand capture mechanisms such as conversion content and warm outbound outreach.

** Original Post Content: 
Most B2B brands do "content marketing" like it's 2016.

Here are 7 shifts to build a "media brand" instead:

1. Acknowledge the shift in B2B content consumption

Your buyers don't go on your website's resources center to read your 
"in-depth articles". Instead they educate themselves in communities, private DM groups, social media, AI overviews, webinars, videos...

2. Broaden the scope of your messaging

While content marketing was tightly linked to your brand's messages, building a media brand means zooming out. In your content, talk about broader topics related to your industry and your audience's day-to-day. (Pro tip: hire gen Z, they master this)

3. Use this formula: Topic x formats

"We already talked about this" should not be in your vocabulary. You need to rely on the repetition effect to build top of mind awareness. And your audience wants to learn about a topic from different angles. So take one topic and decline it into many formats.

4. Start a "big repurposing plan" now

You have thousands of interesting insights sitting in your legacy content formats (blogs, white papers, newsletters). Turn all of this into a knowledge center and repurpose into 2026 formats (infographics, carousels, thought leadership...)

5. Put faces on your brand 

Whether it's founder-led or team-led, you need to give the mic to your in-house experts. They'll represent your brand best in the new content channels (see point 1 of this list). Give them a proper infrastructure (read my post from 2 days ago)

6. Create content upgrades: short-form → long-form → gated content

Yes, you need a lot of zero-click content for awareness and authority (short content in the feed that requires 0 action). But you also need longer form to truly help your audience and gated content to connect your revenue system.

7. Use AI the right way in your content (advanced tip)

30% of your content creation volume should be NET NEW content (NNC) ideated and produced by your team. Your team spends most of their time producing this 30% NNC. And for the rest of their time, they can use AI to repurpose content (70% of the volume)

** Annotation: traditional content marketing is becoming less effective and argues that B2B SaaS companies should think like media companies instead. Pierre emphasizes founder-led content, multi-format repurposing.

** Original Post Content: 
I've published B2B educational content since 2022.

A lot of brands starting content in 2026 make this mistake:

1. They assume their audience logs in to social media to consume complex content.

They think "we need to show we're the experts" so they create content too dense to be discoverable. The content would fit a blog or a white paper, but not social zero-click content.

They forget their audience's behaviors:

- People check their feed between meetings;
- They open one tab to search for specific info;
- They post their own content, and scroll a bit.

But some B2B brands make the exact opposite mistake:

2. They assume their audience wants only broad content like stories.

They think "people don't have enough attention for technical content" so they create content that lacks depth, unique insights, or expertise. 

But the truth is that B2B professionals:

- Want to get a unique POV and narrative;
- Want content that makes their career grow;
- Want actionable insights to fix key problems.

Marketer: "Well, what type of content should we create then?"
Me: "You need to organize your content with 3 levels of complexity"

You can publish about one topic and layer the information across complexity levels:

1. Accessible (but unique) content 
2. Intermediate content to show more depth
3. Advanced content for the "Aha! moments"

I've been doing this for 4 years and it's working well.

It works thanks to the halo effect.

Your audience reading a level 1 piece of content will think "if this is good, the other pieces must be good too" and then deep dive into your expertise.

** Annotation: Pierre argues that successful B2B LinkedIn content should balance accessibility with expertise instead of being either overly technical or overly simplified. He introduces a three-level content model—accessible, intermediate, and advanced—that helps brands educate audiences at different stages while gradually establishing authority. The approach leverages the "halo effect" to encourage deeper engagement with future content.
