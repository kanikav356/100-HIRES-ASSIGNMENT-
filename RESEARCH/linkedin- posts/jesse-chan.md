** Original Post Content: 
I've posted over 1,300 times on LinkedIn in 2026 alone.
Generating 38M+ impressions for clients along the way.
Here are my 13 LinkedIn cheat codes:

1. Post on weekends
→ Sat and Sun are the highest-performing days for content (especially growth posts). Fewer people post, so yours gets the room.

2. Post between 11AM–1PM GMT
→ This window catches the US waking up, Europe at lunch and the Gulf in the evening. One post hits every time zone.

3. 1 post, 1 topic, 1 goal
→ Cram three ideas in and people get confused and scroll. So does LinkedIn. If it can't tell who to show your post to, it shows it to no one.

4. Don't just talk about what you do
→ Yes, LinkedIn wants you mostly in your lane. But people follow people. Post your opinions, the things you care about, the hot takes others are too scared to say.

5. A viral post isn't luck
→ It's Topic × Hook × Visual. Get all three right and the math does the work for you. Get one wrong and the whole thing flops.

6. Your hook is 80% of the job
→ Nobody reads line 5 if line 1 is boring. Spend half your time on the first two lines. The rest writes itself.

7. Make your profile your landing page
→ Someone should know exactly what you do and who you help in 10 seconds. If they can't, they leave. Banner, headline, first line of your About.

8. Max 3 links per post
→ LinkedIn doesn't punish links the way people think. It punishes clutter. Keep it to 3 max, and only if the content earns them.

9. No hashtags
→ They did nothing in 2023 and they do less now. They just make you look like you're trying too hard. Drop them.

10. Use portrait images, not landscape
→ More space means more eyes, more dwell time, more reach. The taller the image, the harder you are to scroll past.

11. Warm up before you post
→ Old but gold. Spend 15 minutes commenting on bigger accounts right before you publish. You show up in their crowd first, then your post goes live to a warmer audience.

12. Repost your best stuff every 90 days
→ Most of your audience never saw it the first time. Your "old" post is brand new to them. Recycle what worked.

13. Write at an 8–10-year-old reading level
→ Short words. Short lines. No jargon. The smartest posts are the easiest to read. If a kid can follow it, a busy C-Suite exec can skim it.

Most people overthink LinkedIn.

Either they pile pressure on it to bring in clients, or they think their professional reputation is on the line.

In a way, that's true.

But the same thinking you'd put into a YouTube video or an Instagram post is all LinkedIn really needs.

Yes, it's as competitive as ever. 
Also harder than ever.

But take even half of these on board, and your content is already better than 90% of what's on here

** Annotation: 13 tactical recommendations for improving LinkedIn performance, covering publishing timing, hook writing, profile optimization, visual formatting, readability, repurposing, and audience engagement. The post emphasizes that consistent growth comes from optimizing multiple elements of the content system rather than relying on algorithm changes or luck.

** Original Post Content: 
It's not the algorithm.

It's your content.

This may sound like brutal news to hear on a Monday morning 😅 

LinkedIn is as fair as it gets.
It's a free market.

You can post anything you want.

And the people who see it decide one thing:

- Is this worth showing to more people, or not?

Good content gets rewarded.
Bad content doesn’t.

Same rules for everyone.

So if your posts aren't performing, there's only one reason:

- The content isn't good enough.

I know. That stings.
But it's actually liberating.

Because you're not fighting a shadowban.

You're not being punished for your niche.
There's no twisted algorithm out to get you.

There's just the market.
And the market is telling you the truth.

Once you accept that, LinkedIn becomes a lot simpler.

Here's how to approach content after accepting this truth:

1. Start with them, not you
→ Nobody clicks for your news.
→ They click when you name a problem they're living in.

2. One topic per post
→ Five ideas in one post means none of them land.
→ Pick one. Drive it home.

3. Be as concise as humanly possible.
→ Every extra word is a reason to scroll away.
→ Cut until it hurts. Then cut once more.

4. Write for dwell, not the like.
→ Reach follows attention held, not applause given.
→ Earn the save and the share. Those are the signals that compound.

The reach isn't being kept from you.
It's being earned by people writing better than you.

Good news?

That's a skill. 
And skills can be fixed.

** Annotation: Jesse Chan argues that LinkedIn success is determined primarily by content quality rather than algorithm changes. She encourages creators to focus on audience problems, maintain a single objective per post, write concisely, and optimize for reader attention rather than superficial engagement metrics. The post reframes LinkedIn growth as a skill that improves through better content rather than platform hacks.


## Post 3

** Date: 1 month ago

** Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jessectchan_how-to-know-what-to-post-on-linkedin-activity-7463141034307362816-qObH?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADVBgGQBmJYgFfekDv8o_mjFVTOunUco2fw

** Original Post Content: 
"What on earth should I post today?"

If you ask yourself this, you're destined for failure.

+ it will turn LinkedIn into your worst-ROI activity

In terms of time + maybe even money.
(especially when you're asking it 30 minutes before posting)

Everyone has an abundance of things to say about what they do. 

You've built something. 
You've made big mistakes. 
You've had wins, frameworks, conversations. 

The shortage isn't material. It's planning.

Last-minute writing is reactive.
And reactive content can't be tied to a goal.

So it can't grow an audience, convert buyers or build a list. 

Whatever your goal, it won’t happen.

It just fills a slot.

3 months in, you wonder why LinkedIn hasn't moved a single business metric.

The fix is better questions.

Asked once a month, not 30 minutes before posting.

Here are the ones I'd start with:

1️⃣ What does my ICP wake up worrying about?
The mistake most people make is asking what their ICP wants. 
Wants are surface-level. 
Worries are deeper; more emotional.
Write to the worry and you'll never have to manufacture urgency.

2️⃣ What did I believe 2 years ago that I now think is wrong?
A change of mind is the highest-leverage angle most people ignore. 
It signals you've actually been in the work long enough to update your views. 

3️⃣ What's a take my industry would push back on?
Safe takes get scrolled past. 
The post that performs is the one half your industry would disagree with.
(Provided you can defend it) 
Look at what gets called "best practice" in your space and ask which parts you'd actually argue against.

4️⃣ What conversation did I have this week that taught me something?
Most of your best content is sitting in your inbox, your DMs and your client calls. 
The line a founder said on Tuesday is worth more than 10 invented insights. 
Keep notes and you'll never run dry.

5️⃣ What's obvious to me but novel to my audience?
Expertise blinds you to what's actually useful. 
The things you assume "everyone knows" are usually the exact things people are paying for. 
Write the basics. Write them well. 

6️⃣ What question do I keep getting asked?
If 3 people asked it this month, 300 are silently wondering. 
Repeat questions reveal what your audience is literally telling you what they want to read.

7️⃣ What does the data in my own work actually show?
Numbers from your own business beat opinions every time. 
Proof > Anything else
The patterns you've spotted across clients, campaigns or projects are content no one else can write. 

Your work is your moat. Use it.

Sit down for an hour with these. 
You'll walk away with a month of content.

Posting without a plan isn't a strategy. 
It's LinkedIn’s version of gambling.

So decide what you want from this platform.
Then reverse-engineer the content that gets you there.

** Annotation: Jesse Chan presents a structured framework for generating LinkedIn content ideas by focusing on audience problems, customer conversations, industry opinions, recurring questions, and proprietary data. She argues that consistent content performance depends on strategic planning rather than spontaneous posting, enabling creators to align content with business objectives.
