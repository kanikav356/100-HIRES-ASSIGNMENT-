** Original Post Content: 
The inflection point that started my "zero click marketing" journey: It was 2014 and I was doing B2B marketing at Fitbit...

We were in the business of employee benefits and corporate wellness. The obvious SEO play was:

- Find the high-volume keywords.
- Create content for them.
- Rank 1st for "corporate wellness," etc.
- Step 4: Profit (I guess?)

But even back then that felt wrong to me.

Because the people searching "what is corporate wellness?" probably weren't the people buying corporate wellness programs.

The buyers already knew the category. They already had internal politics, budget constraints, implementation questions, vendor fatigue, and a million reasons to be skeptical.

So the impactful work wasn't in ranking for the most common beginner keyword. It was to understand the audience well enough to make things that actually met them where they were.

That's the part I didn't have language for yet.

But looking back, it was audience research.

And that's still what informs Zero Click Marketing today.

If you're marketing to an informed audience, you can't just chase low-hanging fruit topics. You have to get on their level. You have to speak their language. You have to understand what they already know, what they're tired of hearing, and what they're still trying to solve.

That work is harder.

It's also the work that earns credibility.

Zero Click Marketing isn't simply to create content for platforms where people already spend time.

** Annotation: Amanda explains that successful B2B content begins with understanding the audience rather than chasing high-volume keywords. Reflecting on her experience at Fitbit, she argues that informed buyers require content addressing their real challenges, decision-making processes, and industry context. This audience-first mindset forms the basis of effective zero-click marketing, where content delivers value directly on the platform instead of relying solely on website traffic.

** Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/amandanat_everyone-got-told-to-10x-their-content-with-activity-7470127608979120129-lj86?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADVBgGQBmJYgFfekDv8o_mjFVTOunUco2fw

** Original Post Content: 
Everyone got told to 10x their content with AI.
 
So now every other post reads like it was steeped with the same lukewarm teabag. Same structure. Same “It’s not this. It’s that.” Same nothing.
 
They're no match for lived experience.
 
A large language model is a phenomenal line cook. Hand it a recipe that already exists online and it'll plate the dish faster and cleaner than you can. What it can't do is taste. 
 
It wasn't on that customer call this morning. It didn’t watch the feature flop and ruminate on why. It has no idea what it actually feels like to do the job — because it has never once done the job.
 
My friend Kaleigh Moore put it plainly on today's episode of Zero Click Marketing: the person who's hands-on with your product, talking to customers, running the experiments — that's a goldmine. Their point of view isn't published anywhere else. It's all firsthand.
 
Which means the most defensible content input you have is the people already on your payroll.

** Annotation: Amanda argues that while AI can accelerate content production, it cannot replace firsthand knowledge gained through customer interactions, product development, and real-world experimentation. She encourages organizations to leverage the expertise of employees closest to customers, positioning original insights as the strongest source of authentic and differentiated content.


## Post 3

** Date: 2 months ago

** Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/amandanat_content-marketing-in-a-post-seo-world-zoom-activity-7455254608920096768-Ccxm?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADVBgGQBmJYgFfekDv8o_mjFVTOunUco2fw

** Original Post Content: 
Content marketing is usually where the business mess becomes visible.

• Unclear positioning results in vague blog posts.
• A fuzzy ICP becomes "content for everyone."
• A weak point of view becomes tips that sound like every other company’s.
• Misaligned business goals become a content calendar full of activity, but not much progress.

And we're starting to see more of that mess getting exposed. It's in the "ultimate guides" losing AI citations, the content that's built to capture traffic instead of creating trust, and in the posts that *technically* rank but don't actually drive business outcomes.

We've even seen it in the HubSpot traffic discourse, too: the viral version was "HubSpot lost 80% of its blog traffic," while the real answer is more nuanced: search traffic is changing and broad traffic was always less important than durable demand.

That’s the interesting part.

Because traffic loss (in general, not just HubSpot) could be revealing something deeper: content that looked successful because it earned visits, but was never tightly connected to the business.

If that's true (and I think it is), you can't just "fix the blog."

You have to get sharper on who you’re for, what your audience needs to understand, what they already believe, and why they should trust you.

That’s the conversation I’m having with Matt Lerner this week.

We’re talking about content marketing as a business function, not just a publishing function — and how to make it create trust, demand, and actual business value in a zero-click world.

** Annotation: Amanda explains that effective content marketing depends on clear positioning, a well-defined audience, and a distinct point of view rather than simply increasing traffic. She argues that the shift toward AI-powered discovery exposes weaknesses in content strategies focused solely on rankings, emphasizing instead the importance of creating trust, demand, and meaningful business outcomes.
