** Original Post Content: 
I'm seeing some of my linkedin posts with links get almost 2x the amount of link clicks vs likes on the post.

This one got 95k impressions.

So the reach on it was pretty nuts but it only got 385 likes which feels pretty low for that amount of impressions.

Here's the even more nuts bit:

The link in the post got 701 clicks.

Almost DOUBLE the likes.

That tells me something that I think everyone who posts on LinkedIn regularly needs to understand:

90%+ of people do not like or comment on anything.

They do not publically engage with things.

They read and engage in private in ways no one can see them, like their boss or their colleague or their friends.

For example, this post was about how I could in theory make $960k/year if I signed 10x ghostwriting clients at $8k/month each.

You're working a regular 9-5 job. 

What does your boss / colleague say if they see you like that post?

You just know when you get to your desk on monday, that's coming up in conversation. Or it's being circulated round the private Teams / Slack channels internally.

So people do private engagement. They click links, they save, they share behind close doors.

THIS IS STILL ENGAGEMENT.

LinkedIn fucking loves this!

So my advice to you if you want more engagement:

1. Put links in your posts
2. Tag company pages in your posts
3. Tag personal profiles in your posts

Get people clicking on things that don't show up publically.

This is the underworld of LinkedIn engagement and it's absolutely wonderful.

** Annotation: The post highlights the importance of private engagement signals such as link clicks, saves, and shares, arguing that many B2B buyers consume content without publicly interacting. Encourages marketers to evaluate LinkedIn performance beyond visible engagement metrics.

** Original Post Content: 
I've written 2,921 LinkedIn posts in 4 years. 

But on Wednesday 15 people watched me scroll my LinkedIn feed live for 60 minutes so they know exactly what I look for when I read posts.

Here’s the 7 biggest takeaways on what I was looking for (+ what I’d change):

1. a strong hook gives me a reason to care before I click “see more”

2. if you make a promise in the hook, you need to cash it in immediately

3. formatting still matters more than people want to admit

4. examples do a lot of the heavy lifting in educational posts

5. a lot of posts underperform because they assume too much context

6. not every post needs to speak to the same stage of awareness

7. commenting is easier when you stop overthinking it

** Annotation: Summarizes practical lessons from analyzing thousands of LinkedIn posts, highlighting the importance of strong hooks, clear structure, contextual writing, examples, and tailoring content to different audience awareness levels.


## Post 3

** Date: 1 week ago

** Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mattjbarker1_last-week-a-guy-told-me-his-ideal-clients-activity-7475517727710003200-hjg9?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADVBgGQBmJYgFfekDv8o_mjFVTOunUco2fw

** Original Post Content: 
Last week, a guy told me his ideal clients never engage on LinkedIn. But then asked why his post's engagement was flat. Well, here's the answer for ya (this will change your WHOLE LinkedIn strategy):

But first for context, this guy's ideal clients are recruiters. 

And recruiters by his own account don't comment, don't like, don't share, don't anything in public because there's a professional cost to every click. Bosses watching, competitors watching etc so they sit on their hands and keep shtum. 

Fair enough.

But he was writing for them and still measuring the success or failure of his LinkedIn posts based on engagement that was literally never going to come.

That is not going to work. Not now, not ever.

Most people on LinkedIn (and any other social media platform) never engage with ANYTHING.

Doesn't matter the niche. 

The people who do are usually your peers and the platform's active users and they engage because they get something back or feel they owe you a comment. That's reciprocity, not love.

So:

- Engagement content is mostly read by peers
- Client content is read by buyers

Expecting your SILENT ideal client to power your engagement is a big ol mistake.

So before you write, decide the job of the post:

- Reach post → written for active peers, judged on reactions
- Client post → written for buyers, judged on DMs, clicks, calls

If your ideal client goes silent, it is not because your content is shit or you're doing something massively wrong.

It's because you're measuring the wrong type of post with the wrong metric.

Likes and clients RARELY come from the same post.

Stop expecting them to.
e.g. these 3 posts below generated 100s of new followers and 100,000s of impressions but zero directly attributable inbound opportunities and im totally fine with that because I know which posts will and which posts wont.

** Annotation: Explains why B2B buyers often consume LinkedIn content without publicly engaging. Introduces the concept of assigning different objectives to posts (reach vs. client acquisition) and emphasizes measuring business outcomes rather than vanity engagement metrics.
